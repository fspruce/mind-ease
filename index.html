Common Issues

Anxiety

A common mental health condition that causes feelings of worry, fear or unease. It can affect your thoughts, body and daily life.

Anxiety

What is Anxiety?

Anxiety can show up as a constant worry, restlessness, or physical symptoms like a racing heart and difficulty sleeping. It may be triggered by specific situations or persist without a clear cause. While occasional anxiety is normal, ongoing symptoms might need support or treatment.

Support and Resources

The NHS offers self-help guides, talking therapies, and practical advice to help manage anxiety. You don't have to face it alone.

Visit the NHS website for further support
Depression

Depression is more than feeling sad - it's a persistent low mood that can affect how you think, feel and function day to day.

Read More

Depression

What is Depression?

Depression can cause deep feelings of hopelessness, fatigue, and loss of interest in things you once enjoyed. It may affect sleep, appetite and concentration, and can make even small tasks feel overwhelming. It's not a sign of weakness - it's a real condition that many people experience.

Support and Resources

The NHS provides guidance on types of depression, coping strategies and treatment options, like therapy and medication. Help is available and recovery is possible.

Visit the NHS website for further support
Stress

Stress is a natural response to pressure or change. While short bursts can help us stay alert, ongoing stress can affect our mood, body and relationships.

Read More

Stress

What is Stress?

Stress happens when we feel overwhelmed or unable to cope with demands. It can be triggered by work, relationships, health issues or sudden life changes. Physical symptoms might include headaches, muscle tension, or trouble sleeping. Emotionally, stress can lead to irritability, anxiety or felling low.

Support and Resources

Managing stress starts with recognising its signs. The NHS offers practical tips, breathing exercises and online tools to help you cope. You don't have to handle it alone - support is available.

Visit the NHS website for further support
Obsessive Compulsive Disorder (OCD)

OCD is a mental health condition involving unwanted thoughts (obsessions) and repetitive behaviours (compulsions). It can be distressing and interfere with daily life.

Obsessive Compulsive Disorder (OCD)

What is OCD?

OCD causes persistent, intrusive thoughts that create anxiety - like fears of harm, contamination, or things being "not right". To ease distress, people may feel compelled to repeat actions like checking, cleaning, or counting. These behaviours can offer temporary relief, but often reinforce the cycle.

Support and Resources

OCD is treatable. The NHS offers talking therapies like CBT, which help break the pattern of obsessions and compulsions. You're not alone - support is available, and recovery is possible.

Visit the NHS website for further support
Eating Disorders

Eating Disorders are serious mental health conditions. They involve using food - either too much or too little - as a way to cope with difficult emotions, stress or self image.

Eating Disorders

What is an Eating Disorder?

Eating disorders aren't just about food. They're about how someone feels inside. People may overeat, undereat, or obsess over food and body image as a way to manage emotional pain, anxiety, or a need for control. These behaviours can feel compulsive and isolating, and they often mask deeper struggles.

Types of Eating Disorders
  • Anorexia: Restricting food intake due to fear of gaining weight or feeling "not good enough".
  • Bulimia: Binge eating followed by purging (e.g. vomiting, laxatives or over-exercising).
  • Binge Eating Disorder: Eating large amounts of food quickly, often in secret, followed by guilt or shame.
  • OSFED & ARFID: Other patterns that don't fit neatly into one category, but still cause distress and impact health.
Support and Resources

You're not alone. The NHS offers support through talking therapies like CBT, and organisations like Beat provide helplines and peer support. Recovery is possible, and help is available.

Visit the NHS website for further support

Sexuality & Gender

Understanding the Struggle

Coming to terms with your sexuality or gender identity can be empowering - but it may also involve confusion, fear, or distress. For some, this icludes experiences like gender dysphoria, where there's discomfort between one's gender identity and assigned sex at birth. Others may feel isolated or anxious when navigating their sexuality, especially if they face stigma or rejection.

Support and Resources

You're not alone. The NHS offers guidance and support for those experiencing gender dysphoria, and promotes inclusive care for LGBTQ+ individuals. Talking therapies, peer support and affirming environments can make a big difference.
